Academic Integrity
What is — and isn't — acceptable when using AI in coursework

✓ Do

  • Use AI to explain concepts you don't fully understand
  • Ask for help with brainstorming and structuring ideas
  • Request examples of good writing or problem-solving approaches
  • Use AI to get feedback on your own draft work
  • Cite AI assistance when required by your institution
  • Use AI to generate practice questions for self-study

✗ Don't

  • Submit AI-generated work as your own writing
  • Use AI on exams or assignments where it is explicitly prohibited
  • Ask AI to write complete essays, reports, or solutions
  • Present AI-generated ideas as your original thoughts without attribution
  • Use AI to bypass learning objectives or avoid engaging with the material
  • Assume AI use is allowed unless it is explicitly permitted
🛡️
Privacy & Data Protection
Protecting your information and others' when using AI tools

✓ Do

  • Use generic placeholders instead of real personal information
  • Assume AI conversations could be stored, reviewed, or used for training
  • Check the privacy policy of any AI tool before use
  • Use your institution's recommended AI tools when available
  • Delete sensitive conversations when possible

✗ Don't

  • Share names, addresses, phone numbers, or student ID numbers
  • Input sensitive research data or confidential information
  • Share login credentials or financial information
  • Discuss private matters involving other people
  • Assume your AI provider won't use your inputs
🔍
Verification & Fact-Checking
Ensuring the accuracy of AI-generated information

✓ Do

  • Verify statistics and facts with credible, primary sources
  • Check that any AI-generated references actually exist
  • Look for consensus among multiple reliable academic sources
  • Use academic databases (Google Scholar, JSTOR) to verify claims
  • Consult your instructor when AI output conflicts with course material

✗ Don't

  • Assume AI is correct because it sounds confident
  • Use AI as your sole or primary source of information
  • Trust AI-generated references without independently verifying them
  • Ignore contradictory information from reliable academic sources
  • Submit facts or statistics sourced only from AI without verification
💬
Prompting Examples
See what good and poor prompts look like in practice

✓ Ethical

  • "Help me create an outline for an essay on renewable energy policy"
  • "What are the main arguments for and against this position?"
  • "How can I improve the structure of this paragraph I wrote?"

✗ Problematic

  • "Write my 1500-word essay on renewable energy policy"
  • "Generate a complete essay I can submit for this assignment"
  • "Write a better version of this essay for me"

✓ Ethical

  • "Explain the steps to solve this type of integral"
  • "What concepts do I need to understand to approach this problem?"
  • "Where did I go wrong in my calculation — here is my working"

✗ Problematic

  • "Solve these 10 calculus problems for my homework"
  • "Give me the answers to this problem set"
  • "Complete this assignment for me"

✓ Ethical

  • "What are the key themes in research on climate adaptation?"
  • "Help me organise my notes from these papers I have read"
  • "Suggest search terms I could use to find papers on this topic"

✗ Problematic

  • "Write a literature review for my research paper"
  • "Summarise these papers so I don't have to read them"
  • "Generate a bibliography for my thesis"
🏛️
Institutional policies vary. These guidelines are general best practices. Always check your specific course handbook, syllabus, or speak to your instructor to understand what AI use is permitted for each assignment.
