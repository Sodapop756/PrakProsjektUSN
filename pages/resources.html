Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions students ask about using AI in their studies.

Using AI is not inherently cheating — it depends entirely on how you use it and what your institution's policies say. Using AI to understand concepts, brainstorm ideas, or get feedback on your own work is generally acceptable. Submitting AI-generated work as your own, especially without permission or proper citation, is typically considered academic dishonesty. When in doubt, always ask your instructor before submitting.
Yes, in most cases. Most academic institutions now require you to acknowledge AI assistance. Specific citation formats vary — APA, Harvard, and Chicago all have emerging standards for AI tools. Typically you should mention the tool used, the date of access, and your prompt. Always check your institution's specific guidelines, as requirements are still evolving rapidly.
Always cross-reference important information with credible, primary sources. Use academic databases (Google Scholar, JSTOR), peer-reviewed journals, government publications, and reputable educational sources. Look for consensus among multiple reliable sources. Be especially careful with statistics and citations — AI tools can produce convincing-looking references that do not actually exist.
Never share: your full name, home address, phone number, student ID or national identification number, financial information, passwords, health information, or other people's personal data. Assume that anything you input to an AI tool could be stored, reviewed by the provider, or used for model training. This also applies to sensitive research data — even anonymised data — without explicit permission.
This depends entirely on your institution's and instructor's policies. Some courses allow AI as a study aid but not for graded work. Some have specific rules per assignment type. Policies are changing rapidly, so what was allowed last semester may have changed. When in doubt, always ask your instructor for written clarification before submitting.
Hallucination is when an AI confidently produces false information — including invented citations, fabricated statistics, or made-up events. It is particularly dangerous because AI tools do not signal uncertainty the way a person would. To spot it: verify every specific claim against an original source, check that any citations actually exist in a database, and be especially sceptical when AI sounds very confident about obscure details.
Use AI as a tutor, not an answer machine. Ask for explanations of concepts you don't understand. Request analogies that connect new ideas to things you already know. Ask for practice problems and give it your own attempt to get feedback on. Get it to outline — not write — essays. Ask "why" and "how" questions rather than "what is the answer." The goal is deeper understanding of the material, not completion of the task.
